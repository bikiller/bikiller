MUFG strategist Derek Halpenny argues that the US blockade in the Strait of Hormuz is creating a meaningful inflation shock risk for both the US and the global economy. Oil prices are climbing alongside broader input costs, as the disruption in this critical shipping chokepoint tightens energy supply and amplifies inflation pressures. The development is also reshaping currency market sentiment toward the USD.

In forex trading, rising inflation expectations are reinforcing safe-haven demand, keeping the USD supported in the near term, though concerns over weaker US growth may cap gains over time. Major currency pairs such as EUR/USD and USD/JPY are increasingly sensitive to shifting rate expectations, as markets reassess Federal Reserve policy in response to energy-driven inflation and growing stagflation risks.

For traders, the shock is driving heightened volatility across FX and commodities markets, with oil-linked currencies and import-dependent economies facing the greatest pressure. Rising input costs risk widening trade imbalances and weighing on EUR and Asian FX against the USD. Overall, tensions in the Strait of Hormuz remain a key catalyst for short-term exchange rate swings and broader risk sentiment.
