Oil price spikes, driven by the ongoing Iran conflict, are exerting pressure on most Asian currencies, as many regional economies rely heavily on oil imports. The sustained rise in crude prices has worsened the outlook for currencies like the Korean Won (KRW), Indian Rupee (INR), and Philippine Peso (PHP). With oil prices climbing, these currencies face increased trade deficits and inflationary pressures, further weakening their exchange rates against major currencies such as the USD.

Meanwhile, some currencies appear more resilient to the oil shock. The Chinese Yuan (CNH) and Malaysian Ringgit (MYR) have shown relative stability, supported by stronger trade balances and economic fundamentals. Despite regional vulnerabilities, the global demand for oil and the USD’s influence on Asian trading markets continue to shape the performance of these currencies. The market remains sensitive to oil fluctuations, with traders closely monitoring geopolitical risks tied to the Iran crisis.
