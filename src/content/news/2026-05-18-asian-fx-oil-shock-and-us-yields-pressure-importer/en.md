Asian emerging-market currencies are coming under pressure as a combination of rising US real yields, a stronger dollar, and elevated oil prices weigh on sentiment. Traders have noted depreciation across major currency pairs involving the USD, with the greenback gaining against regional importers. MUFG’s Michael Wan points out that the shift reflects concerns over higher financing costs and import bills amid persistent oil volatility.  

The US dollar index (DXY) remains supported by expectations of sustained US yield strength, pressuring local exchange rates in Asia. Economists warn that currencies such as the Indonesian rupiah, Thai baht, and Philippine peso may face additional weakness if crude prices stay elevated, raising import costs and inflation risks. These dynamics are prompting cautious trading, with investors increasingly favoring USD-denominated assets for relative safety.  

Market participants are closely watching technical levels as Asian FX markets navigate the dual pressures of energy shocks and US monetary conditions. Spot exchange rates are showing heightened volatility, with short-term trading strategies focusing on hedging against continued USD strength. Analysts suggest that unless oil prices stabilize or US yields ease, regional currencies could struggle to regain ground, impacting cross-border trade flows and investment sentiment.
