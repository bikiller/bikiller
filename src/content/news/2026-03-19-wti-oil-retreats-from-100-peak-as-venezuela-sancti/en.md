West Texas Intermediate (WTI) crude eased back from an intraday peak above **$100** to trade around **$97.20 per barrel**, sliding about **1.68%** on Thursday as traders weighed signs of improving supply against ongoing geopolitical risks. Markets have been buoyed by the U.S. easing sanctions on Venezuelan oil exports, potentially adding barrels to global flows and tempering recent strength in energy-linked assets. With the U.S. dollar still firm, currency pairs like **USD/CAD** and **USD/NOK** reflected oil’s pullback, influencing broader commodity-linked FX trading.

Despite the pullback, persistent tensions in the Middle East continue to cloud the outlook for supply, keeping risk premiums elevated and fueling volatile trading in both crude and foreign exchange markets. The surge in oil prices has had knock‑on effects for exchange rates such as **EUR/USD** and **GBP/USD**, as higher energy costs feed into inflation expectations and central bank pricing models.
